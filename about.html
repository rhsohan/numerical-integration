Educational Mission

The Numerical Integration Solver & Learning Platform was developed to bridge the gap between abstract mathematical derivations and interactive visual applications. Numerical integration forms the core of many engineering designs, scientific computing codes, and statistical modeling tools. Yet, students often struggle to visualize how partitioning intervals affects approximation accuracy.

Our platform resolves this by combining:

  • Pedagogical Explanations: Detailed step-by-step arithmetic pathways showing how limits, step sizes, and discrete ordinate tables feed directly into composite formulas.
  • Dynamic Shading: Visualizations mapping out approximating shapes (rectangles, trapezoids, parabolas, and cubics) right under the continuous function trace.

Academic Context

Calculations on this website follow the standard closed-form Newton-Cotes quadrature formulations. These methods assume that a function $f(x)$ is sampled at $n+1$ points distributed uniformly across $[a, b]$. We support:

1. Linear Interpolation

The Trapezoidal Rule maps straight lines between adjacent coordinates, modeling the area as $n$ individual trapezoids. It is the most robust rule but requires smaller intervals ($h$) for high accuracy.

2. Quadratic Interpolation

Simpson's 1/3 Rule maps quadratic parabolas through groups of three coordinates. It requires $n$ to be an even number, yielding significantly lower error rates for smooth curves.

3. Cubic Interpolation

Simpson's 3/8 Rule maps cubic curves through groups of four coordinates. It requires $n$ to be a multiple of 3 and is ideal for representing higher-order curved fields.
